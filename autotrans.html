Exploratory Data Analyses

Figure 1 below shows how cars’ miles per gallon (MPG) distributes out over the number of cylinders. From the figure, it appears that there is a negative correlation between the number of cylinders and the MPG and that an automatic transmission delivers better gas mileage than a manual transmission for 4 and 6 cylinder engines.

However, the number of cylinders can really be thought of as a factor variable or ‘bin’ variable for engine size. Typically a larger engine will will have more cylinders. And, typically an larger engine (as measured by engine displacement in Cubic Inches), will provide more power to the car.

Figure 2 below shows cars’ MPG as a function of three possible predictors: engine displacement (size), vehicle weight and engine horsepower. In this study, we want to answer whether a car with an automatic transmission gets different MPG than a car with manual transmission. From Figure 2, a negative correlation between MPG and vehicle weight (center graph) is readily apparent. However, the weight of the vehicle determines the number of horsepower needed to propel the vehicle at a customer acceptable level of performance. The engine size is really more of a proxy for engine horsepower, so for purposes of this study, we’ll use engine horsepower as the predictor variable for the MPG response.

Appendix A1: Reader-friendly transformation of the mtcars data.frame

This R code adds columns that map the transmission type in the source data to the terms “Automatic” and “Manual”. It also interprets the vs column to be the engine type: V-block or Inline.

data("mtcars")

mtcars2=mtcars
mtcars2$tran.type=factor(ifelse(mtcars$am==1,"Automatic","Manual"))
#0 means V, 1 means straight
mtcars2$eng.type = factor(ifelse(mtcars$vs == 1,"Inline","V-Block"))
